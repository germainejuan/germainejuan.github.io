I struggled in the beginning of taking the ICS 314 Software Engineering class at UH Manoa. Every week on Thursdays, we do an in-class WOD (Workout of the Day) that would be worth 100 points. It was an all-or-nothing type of assignment. You have to either get it or you don’t, you either get 100/100 or 0/100; there are no partial points. For the first few weeks of the semester, we worked on Javascript assignments. I struggled with Javascript, as I have limited to no experience with Javascript prior to the class. I failed four WODS, one of the WODs I purposely skipped because I struggled with the lesson that week, since I was stressed out with adjusting from working on JSFiddle to IntelliJ. I guess what I needed was to put in a little more effort into the class, as Professor Johnson recommended that we gave ourselves two hours a day to review materials for this class. Afraid that I would not be able to pass the class, I tried my best in the following weeks to pass the weekly in-class WODs to improve my grades and avoid failing. Fortunately for me, the following assignments involved HTML, which I had prior experience with, so from there on, I was able to pass the rest of the in-class WODs that were conducted on Thursdays.
## User Interface Frameworks
My favorite module for the class was when we started implementing Semantic UI to improve the visual style with the HTML and CSS code that we were working with in previous modules. Semantic UI is basically a user interface for web applications. User interfaces are basically pre-made templates of tools that are designed to keep a website visually organized and appealing. These basically make websites look like they weren’t made in the 90’s.

I had prior experience with HTML before taking ICS 314 Software Engineering, but have not done work with CSS. Having learned about HTML and CSS and moving on to User Interface Frameworks, I got to witness how modern web applications are built.

Why not code from scratch? Why bother using UI Frameworks? Well, I’d prefer using UI Frameworks mostly because it is easier for a programmer to use an already existing and well-documented tool so that others are capable of looking at it. It also reduces the code that you have to write and makes your code look tidier since you are mostly importing pre-existing code for the tools needed for the website. It also creates a standardized look that flows seamlessly throughout the site.
## Ethics in Software Engineering … but for whom?
I am a Computer Science major, but I am also an Ethnic Studies major. As someone who is involved in cultural and political work outside of my STEM classes, I would say that my values and morals are a big part of what motivates me to do things for myself and my community.

My biggest fear is that one day my politics will prevent me from obtaining a career in STEM. The best way to describe my stance is Anti-Imperialism. I see imperialism in the colonization of my homeland by the U.S. and the neocolonial policies today. I see imperialism in the migration of my family to obtain “better” economic opportunities just to have our labor exploited on the stolen land of another nation. I see imperialism in the use of University of Hawaiʻi resources to develop Agent Orange. I see imperialism when the U.S. military occupies so much land when Native Hawaiians aren’t able to live on their ancestral land and in the settler-colonial government who make choices that prioritizes profit over people. I see imperialism when a lot of the careers that are advertised to STEM students in the University of Hawaiʻi are somehow connected to the military or the military industrial complex.

When we had our discussion in class regarding the ethics in software engineering, we examined the case study of University of Minnesota researchers conducting experiments on the Linux kernel. I found it interesting how a group, assigned to justify that the researchers were ethical, have said that they were ethical because those experiments have not been conducted on human beings after having read the ACM Code of Ethics and Professional Conduct. Though that statement may be true, there are going to be maintainers and developers who would clean up the unnecessary mess that they have made. If you see a custodian on campus grounds cleaning up an area, would you go up to them and spill your trash in front of them on purpose because it’s their job to do so?

I find it disturbing that a lot of technology today is used for malicious acts. Maybe Facebook was not intended to destabilize political spheres of certain countries from fake news, but they have the capability of doing so. Information is spread through the internet, but since the Internet is vast with an infinite amount of information flowing through every second, it is hard to control and maintain information going through it. Is Facebook ethical? I would ask more specific questions, such as… 
What type of information is Facebook allowing on their platform? 
What type of information is Facebook taking down?
What is Facebook doing with my personal information?
Where is my personal information going?
Why does social media flag media from other countries but don’t flag media that promote American propaganda?
What is social media’s role in spreading propaganda and manufacturing consent from the public?
And more...

Going back to the topic of imperialism, I find it suspicious that the topic of imperialism is not often brought up in regards to ethics concerning STEM communities in the U.S… Why is it so awkward to bring up politics in STEM communities? Does the ethic of avoiding harm (ACM 1.2) only apply to privileged groups and nations? I’m curious why computing ethics are only asked when it comes to computing projects. No one ever brings up the fact that careers in tech being concentrated in a certain area leads to gentrification. Ultimately, I think my point here is that code of ethics for programmers should be extended beyond computing projects and how people who work in tech can possibly further contribute to marginalized people’s oppression beyond computers.
